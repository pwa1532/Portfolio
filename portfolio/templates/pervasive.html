Pervasive Study Spaces

SI 612 - Pervasive Interaction Design

Project Proposal
North Quad is the newest building at the University of Michigan and is a unique combination of academic offices, classrooms, and dorm hall. As such, the building is designed with dozens of lounges for both academic and recreational use. This has cause some trouble in the community in that some students to study while others are using the lounges to socialize. My team proposes creating a system to monitor the useage of the lounges so students can easily find the right space for their needs. For a more indepth look at the proposal or the project itself, check out the project website.

Initial Design and Formative Study
The initial concept involved creating a heat map of the build showing how busy specific locations in the building were. After interviewing several students and residents, we refined the idea to focus on a specific subset of the lounges, specifically those found within the dorm portion of the building. We also found that students want to more then just how busy a room is, they want to know how lound it is and if any of their friends are in it.


In the above concept story of how our project. The protagonist is in a lounge studying when a loud group of students arrives. Obviously she is not very happy with this situation and decides to leave and find a quieter place to study. She wonders around for a while and finds another lounge but it too is full of people talking. She then remembers the that she can check online how busy the lounges are. She pulls out her phone and finds a lounge that fits her needs.

Needs Evaluation and User Enactments
As a continued effort to understand our users, we performed a series of need evaluations and user enactments. The first step in this process was to create a series of scenarios for users. We created three such scenarios: finding your friends in the lounges, finding a quiet place to study, and one about privacy concerns a user might have about their location being made public. A series of potential users were then shown sketches outlining user interaction with the system in the forementioned scenarios. They were given ample time to study the interactions and then asked a series of questions about how they felt about the scenario.


The ntext steps in the process was to get the users involved in the project themselves. In order to do this, a series of paper prototypes were create mimicing he various platforms the system could be omplemented: various tv monitors located throughout the building, website, or smartphone app. The users were then asked to perform various tasks related based on the storyborads and then asked how they felt performing said tasks. This gave the team valuable insight into what users thought was useful and what they could and could not see themselves doing.

Interactive Prototypes
The final stage of the development process involved creating interactive prototypes to put in front of users and test. Three prototypes were developed, one for the various tv monitors around the building, a webpage, and finally a mobile app version. All three versions were then presented to users and tested. Each users was asked simple questions about their initial impression of the system and asked to perform four tasks using a simple speak alound procedure followed by a short post session debriefing.

Final Proposal
The last step in the process was to present our idea to various faculty members and building administrative staff. The group was given a live demenstration of the prototypes and the potential benefits of the system. Three versions of the system were then proposed (bronze, silver, and gold) with various features and costs.
